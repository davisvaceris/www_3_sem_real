Security

Spark's chief cyber security officer Jason Hartman says "the documents being harvested are regarded as the most sensitive and valuable of the information in the government's arsenal" and says organisations are paying the price for not being cyber secure.
He explains: "Businesses cannot afford to underestimate the threat of cyber attack and cyber security is the new normal. As cyber security problems continue to grow and cyber threats become more sophisticated, cyber security is more important than ever."
Statistics show that one in five companies are constantly attacked by hackers.

Think utilities with insecure systems that lack security protocols, unmanaged remote administration protocols (RADIUS, Accounts, NTRPM) and firewalls. Think many thousands of file servers where ROAS does not account for how many servers can hide and spot on the Internet. Think UNC access points that no more than a generic email server may detect. Very few, maybe no customers may lift software in bulk. SMBv1 provides all that and it outperforms risky file server apps. SMBv2 does not provide all that, but it is more secure than outdated NTRPM and Basic Authentication, and constantly improving.

Consider the fact that switches are the chosen security device – switches run UEFI, so each software account is ephemeral in setting up, only to be changed by next reboot. And switches can be found through the industry and routed by industry radar or Internet segmentation technology, so while an attacker or intruder may be able to flash upwards for errant outgoing packets, that is not the case for other networks through which an administrator may route cables that are critical, safety sensitive, or domain backdoor into others – cloud networks, banking -- after having dropped the box outside the firewall.

• Update SMB2 to SMB3.1+. SMBv2.1/2+ handles public networks safely while providing easy credential-based management through any modern firewall platform.

• Be absolutely sure you are using an approved UDP stack. Don't waste letters when letters and firewall software are available that you can support. Uploading libraries and running third-party hardware drivers is unsafe. Choose the correct platform. For sure use SMITA. (Not SMITECH.) Build with reasonable confidence that you can manage the item, document the steps, and set long-lasting password-protected software accounts. Remember the critical caveat – while customers may log into equipment part of their networks, they certainly may not log into equipment that comes to work on their networks – going through an incompatible, inefficient and cost-regulated firewall license is easy risk to lose otherwise. Unnecessary spreadsheets and half microwages collected at physical infrastructure -- or worse, a wiping of controllers to be placed back through the firewalls upon module removal and inspection – should not make security decisions! At rest, controllers can be programmed for tenacious resets to backups at the network appliance or server network segmentation. Blow-forward publishing away is dangerous. Write scripts based on point-in-time transient information from the remote device during automation.
